Our group works at the intersection of environmental chemistry and engineering, turning fundamental understanding of pollutants into practical, affordable technologies that protect water resources and public health. Three themes drive our work.

Destroying Emerging Contaminants

PFAS — the "forever chemicals" — resist almost everything we throw at them, which is exactly why they accumulate in our water and bodies. We develop methods that don't just move these pollutants around but actually break them apart, cleaving the tough carbon–fluorine bonds at their core. Our hydrothermal alkaline treatment (HALT) work has shown that even the most persistent PFAS can be fully destroyed, and the approach is now being scaled for real-world site cleanups.

Recovering Value from Waste

"Waste" is often a resource in the wrong place. We design processes to recover valuable materials — including lithium and rare earth elements — from end-of-life sources such as spent lithium-ion batteries and solar panels. The goal is a cleaner, more circular materials economy that reduces both pollution and the demand for new mining.

Seeing the Invisible: Environmental Analytical Chemistry

You can't manage what you can't measure. Many emerging contaminants exist at vanishingly low levels and slip past conventional methods. We build and refine analytical approaches — from smarter sample preparation to total organic halogen (TOX) analysis and high-resolution mass spectrometry — so that contaminants can be detected, identified, and tracked accurately.
